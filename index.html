The Brief Story

Our friend Nicholas Canonico, 27?, from Saugerties, NY, is suffering from a severe case of chronic neurological Lyme disease. He is in desperate need for medical attention, but is unable to afford it. He needs your help to support his medical treatment fees.

Donate to the fund for Nick’s Lyme Treatment [link]

Because of his condition, Nick has been unable to work and is on Medicaid. However, Medicaid will not cover doctor fees and treatments outside of their treatment guidelines for Lyme. He has been through the standard intravenous antibiotic procedure allowed by the Center for Disease Control. The first time was for 1 month and the second for 3 months, but the treatments have been unsuccessful.

He is currently trying alternative treatments , but alternative medicine is not covered by Medicaid. Due to the severity of his illness, he has to visit the doctor twice a week and requires weekly supplements. He has a kind and generous doctor who has lowered the costs, but it is still pricy. The goal is to raise about $15,000 to help him pay for his doctor visits and medicine over the course of three years.

At the end of Nick’s treatment, any remaining funds will be donated to institutions doing research on Lyme disease and its treatment.

The Detailed Story

In 2011 Nick was diagnosed with Lyme. When he first went to the doctors, the blood results came back negative, so the doctors told him he didn’t have Lyme. He still wasn’t feeling well, so he went back to the doctors a second time,and they did a more accurate blood test, Western Blot, which determined he had Lyme disease. He was given oral antibiotics, but the antibiotics didn’t fully resolve his symptoms, though he felt much relief.

In 2012, he began to develop more serious symptoms like chronic fatigue, swollen glands, and nasal congestion, but he didn’t connect them with Lyme. When seeing doctors for these symptoms, he wasn’t treated properly.

By the spring of 2013, he began to have trouble thinking, so he went to the doctors again and had a spinal tap done. It confirmed that the Lyme had spread to his brain, causing him to have neurological Lyme disease. He was given an intravenous treatment of antibiotics for 28 days. This procedure requires a PICC line (peripherally inserted central catheter) which is a form of intravenous tube that was inserted into his arm through to his chest and would pump antibiotics into his body daily. The treatment was working, but because the standard treatment regulated by the Center for Disease Control only allows 28 days, he was not given any more and was told he was cured from Lyme. During the fall and winter of 2013, he still wasn’t feeling well so he went back to the doctors and they told him they couldn’t do anything else. He had received the standard treatment for Lyme and regardless of how he felt he was dismissed as not having Lyme.

During the summer of 2014, he severely relapsed. He was administered another spinal tap and again put on the same IV antibiotics for another month. This time around, he visited another doctor who was able to extend his antibiotics for another 2 months. After 3 months of being on IV antibiotics, he still was not feeling well. The Lyme had built up a resistance to the antibiotics from the first treatment in 2013, so it did not work.

In the winter of 2014, he began to take herbal supplements, but they have been still unsuccessful. Because of his condition, he has been unable to work and is on Medicaid. However, Medicaid will not cover doctor fees and treatments outside of their treatment guidelines for Lyme, which have all been unsuccessful in treating his condition.

He is currently trying alternative treatments , but alternative medicine is not covered by Medicaid. Due to the severity of his illness, he has to visit the doctor twice a week and requires weekly supplements. He has a kind and generous doctor who has lowered the costs, but it is still pricy. The goal is to raise about $15,000 to help him pay for his doctor visits and medicine over the course of three years.

At the end of Nick’s treatment, any remaining funds will be donated to institutions doing research on Lyme disease and its treatment.
